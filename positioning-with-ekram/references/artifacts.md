# Artifact Playbooks

Use this reference before producing any named artifact. Each playbook gives the structure, the limits, and what kills it. The position must already be sharp; these playbooks turn a position into copy, they do not rescue a weak one.

## Homepage Hero

Parts, in order:

1. Headline: one claim. It answers "what is it" or "why this," never both halfway. The four questions compressed until one remains.
2. Subhead: the who and the substitute. This is where "for [audience]" and "unlike [substitute]" live.
3. Proof strip: prove before you say. Numbers, named customers, or a benchmark the visitor can check.
4. CTA: the action that matches what actually happens next.

What kills a hero:

- any banned word
- a claim the demo or the pricing page contradicts
- three messages compressed into one headline
- the category name doing all the work ("The analytics platform")
- proof the visitor cannot verify

Test: show it to a stranger for five seconds. If they cannot say what it is and who it is for, the hero failed, no matter how good it sounds. This is the legible test, applied.

## Landing Page

A full page, not just the hero. Sections, in order:

1. Hero: per the homepage hero playbook above.
2. Trust strip: who shows up. The list is the positioning: logos, names, numbers, or the source it is built on.
3. What this is / what this is not: two short columns. The "is not" column is the sacrifice made visible; it does more convincing than the "is" column.
4. What's inside: the concrete contents. For a product: the capabilities that matter. For a book or method: parts, chapters, or the spine. Specific nouns, no feature soup.
5. Proof or source-integrity contract: how the claims are backed. Benchmarks, case studies, or (for books and methods) how sources are verified and what was not invented. This section earns the right to everything above it.
6. Sample lines: let the thing speak. Two or three verbatim excerpts, quotes, or screenshots. Chosen for repeatability, not comprehensiveness.
7. Primary CTA: one action, stated as the outcome. One ask is the sacrifice applied to the page itself.
8. Secondary CTA: one smaller action for the not-yet-convinced (follow, sample, newsletter). More asks than these two is a sacrifice refused.

Rules:

- the page must read as an argument top to bottom: claim, believers, boundaries, contents, proof, taste, action
- the "is not" column and the proof section are the two most skipped and the two most persuasive; the "is not" column is the sacrifice doing the convincing

What kills a landing page: five CTAs, a wall of features where the proof should be, testimonials that praise the team instead of the outcome, and a hero that the rest of the page never backs up.

## Launch Post Or Thread

Structure:

1. Hook: tension, an enemy, or a number. Never "we're excited to announce."
2. Problem signal: the pain, made visible in one or two lines.
3. What it is: one sentence, the positioning statement compressed.
4. Proof: the strongest single piece. A benchmark, a demo clip, a named user.
5. Future delta: what becomes different if this wins, one line.
6. Coordination line: the sentence believers can repeat verbatim.
7. CTA: one action, not three.

What kills a launch post:

- a superlative with no proof in the same post (dunk risk: one screenshot ends it)
- announcing the company instead of the change in the world
- length: if the proof is strong the post is short
- hedging ("we think", "we hope", "our humble attempt")

Test: could a follower retweet line 6 alone and have it make sense? If not, there is no coordination signal.

When the launch runs as a thread, the method still governs it, not thread technique: the coordination signal gets its own line with no link so believers can quote it clean, the sacrifice is stated as refusal because sacrifice is the positioning made visible, proof precedes claims because you prove before you say, and every superlative is checked for dunk risk before it ships. A thread is phased inevitability compressed; if it does not carry the position, no amount of hook craft saves it.

## One-Liner And Bio

Formula:

```text
[Name] [verb]s [outcome] for [who], without [substitute pain].
```

Rules:

- bio: it must survive a profile page on its own, with no thread of context under it
- lead with what it does, not what it is made of
- the name-is-the-thesis check: if the name already says it, do not repeat it
- one claim only; a bio with two claims has none

What kills a one-liner:

- job-title soup ("founder | builder | investor | dreamer")
- "building the future of X"
- category jargon the target reader does not use
- describing the technology instead of the outcome

## Deck Narrative Arc

Slide order for a positioning-led deck:

1. Old world: the assumption everyone still operates on
2. The break: what changed (the why-now force)
3. What it is: the positioning statement, alone on the slide
4. Why this wins: the visible axis (Jordan) or the category definition (Thiel)
5. Proof: the 3 to 5 examples from the proof spine
6. Who shows up: customers, coalition, believers (the list is the positioning)
7. Sacrifice: what you refuse, stated as focus
8. Future delta: the world if this wins
9. The ask

Rules:

- every slide maps to a stage of the method; a slide that maps to no stage is filler
- the nine headlines read in a row must argue the position on their own, old world to ask; that is narrative singularity, one story with no competing versions

What kills a deck: a market-size slide doing the work the position should do, and a "competition" quadrant where you sit top-right for reasons no rival would accept.

## Category Memo

For Thiel-path work only. Parts:

1. Category name: a noun phrase, three words or fewer, that explains or compresses (never merely decorates)
2. Definition: one sentence a stranger could repeat
3. What it replaces: the old category, framed early-not-evil
4. The category question: the question the whole market now has to answer
5. Who else is in: name adjacent players; a category of one is suspicious
6. Education plan: the three pieces of content that teach the category
7. The line: the coordination signal for believers

What kills a category memo:

- a category name that is a synonym for an existing one
- defining the category so narrowly only you fit
- claiming the category before any proof the problem exists

## Book Or Artifact Launch

Books, field manuals, reports, and public artifacts behave differently from startups. The product is finished at launch; the position is the reason to open it.

Parts:

1. Repeatable line: position by the maxim, not the table of contents. One sentence a reader can repeat to someone who will never read the book. This is the whole marketing engine.
2. Who it is for: the specific reader, named by their situation ("for founders who have proof but no story"), and who it is not for. A book for everyone is a book for no one.
3. Mechanism: what the book actually does to the reader. Systems, method, sequence. A mood is not a mechanism.
4. Source or proof contract: what the claims rest on and what was not invented. For curated or reconstructed work, say whose thinking it is, how quotes were verified, and where the compiler's synthesis begins. Integrity is the moat; one fabricated quote ends it.
5. One primary action: read, download, or share. Pick one. A launch that asks for five things gets none.

Launch sequence (compressed phased inevitability):

1. The line, alone, before the artifact
2. Sample pages or excerpts that prove the taste
3. The artifact, free or priced, with the source contract visible
4. Reader evidence: quotes, screenshots of use, who showed up

What kills a book launch: leading with the page count, positioning by topic instead of by line, hiding the strongest excerpt, and launching to no list with no coalition (the list is the positioning).

## Founder Story

Founder stories are proof carriers, not biography. Use only supplied facts.

Parts:

1. Wound: what the founder personally encountered, using the user's nouns.
2. Earned insight: what the wound taught that outsiders miss.
3. Refusal: what the founder will no longer accept or build for.
4. Product position: the thing built because of that refusal.
5. Proof: customer count, names, metric, or shipped evidence supplied by the user.

Rules:

- If a beat is missing, leave it out or ask for one real detail after the draft.
- Do not invent scenes, customer feelings, incident mechanics, dialogue, dates, or inner monologue.
- The story must land on the product's market position, not on founder hero worship.
- If the supplied story is thin, write a plain version and ask for the one missing real detail that would sharpen it.
- Point-back check, before shipping: reread the story one sentence at a time and point each sentence to the user's words it restates. Delete any sentence you cannot point to. "Silent API changes" licenses the word "silent"; it does not license "no changelog, no email, no warning", "nothing broke loudly", or how anyone found out. A shorter true story beats a fuller invented one every time.

What kills a founder story: fictional color, vague resilience language, a heroic arc with no product position, and proof that was not supplied.

## Naming

The name is the thesis. A name either argues the position or it is decoration, and decoration is dead weight.

Tests for any candidate:

- explain, compress, or provoke: which one does it do? If none, it decorates, and it is dead weight
- say it out loud in a sentence: "we use X for that" must sound natural, or the name fails the legible test
- dunk risk: what is the obvious joke, and can you live with it?

Present a few candidates, never a list of twenty, each in the four-part line from the manual: Name. explain / compress / provoke. Thesis. Risk.
